# A1 · Unit 1 · Lesson 1 — Greetings & Introductions
### *Sich vorstellen* — Meet someone and introduce yourself

**Level:** A1 (absolute beginner) · **Unit:** 1 "Hallo!" · **Lesson:** 1 of 5
**Capability:** *Meet someone: say hello, give your name, ask theirs, and say goodbye.*
**Duration:** ~9 min · **New items:** 10 · **Grammar pattern:** 1 (*heißen*: ich heiße / du heißt)
**Status:** Reference implementation — the gold standard for all future lessons.
**Authored against:** LESSON_BLUEPRINT · LESSON_FLOW · VOCABULARY/GRAMMAR/EXERCISE/SPEAKING/FEEDBACK/MICROCOPY/AI_TUTOR_GUIDELINES · A1_SCOPE.

> **Note on scope:** A1_SCOPE splits greetings and introductions across two lessons. This reference lesson intentionally teaches them as **one first-encounter capability** — the smallest *useful* unit of "meeting someone." It stays within 10 items and one pattern.

---

## Learning objective

By the end, a complete beginner can, unaided:
- **greet** someone (Hallo / Guten Tag),
- **introduce themselves** (Ich heiße …),
- **ask another person's name** (Wie heißt du?),
- **respond warmly** (Freut mich · Gut, danke),
- **say goodbye** (Tschüss).

…**without memorizing grammar rules** — the one pattern (*ich heiße / du heißt*) is *discovered*, not lectured.

## Success criteria (this lesson)
1. **Completable** by a zero-German learner without external help.
2. Learner can **produce** "Hallo, ich heiße [name]. Wie heißt du?" from memory.
3. Learner **recognizes** all 10 items in the Mini Review.
4. Learner **feels** "I can actually introduce myself" (not "I finished a lesson").
5. Items are **seeded to SRS**; the learner is set up to remember tomorrow.

## Teaching rationale (overview)
- **Dialogue-first** authoring: the conversation was written first; vocabulary and the pattern were extracted from it (LESSON_BLUEPRINT §0).
- **Formulaic before grammatical:** learners say *Ich heiße* as a whole chunk *before* meeting verb conjugation abstractly — confidence and communication first (A1_SCOPE §4, order 1).
- **Comprehensible input → recognition → light production:** listen/read → flip cards → recognition & cued exercises → one optional micro-production. Difficulty rises monotonically; the lesson ends on wins.
- **Everything the learner sees is usable in a real first meeting.** No filler.

## STAGE 2 — Dialogue (~1.5 min)  *Listen + read*

**Scene:** *Two people meet for the first time.* (Captions always visible; 🔊 normal + 0.5×; **English hidden by default**, one-tap per line.)

| # | Speaker | German | 🔊 Pronunciation (beginner) | English *(tap to reveal)* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Anna | **Hallo!** | HAH-loh | Hello! |
| 2 | Ben | **Hallo! Ich heiße Ben. Wie heißt du?** | HAH-loh · ish HY-suh Ben · vee HYST doo | Hello! My name is Ben. What's your name? |
| 3 | Anna | **Ich heiße Anna. Freut mich!** | ish HY-suh Anna · froyt mish | My name is Anna. Nice to meet you! |
| 4 | Ben | **Freut mich auch. Wie geht's?** | froyt mish owkh · vee gayts | Nice to meet you too. How are you? |
| 5 | Anna | **Gut, danke. Und dir?** | goot · DAHN-kuh · oont deer | Good, thanks. And you? |
| 6 | Ben | **Auch gut. Tschüss, Anna!** | owkh goot · CHÜSS | Also good. Bye, Anna! |
| 7 | Anna | **Tschüss, Ben!** | CHÜSS | Bye, Ben! |

**Reading guidance (on-screen, muted):** *"Tap any line to hear it again or see the English. Don't worry about every word — you'll meet the key ones next."*

**Rationale:** 7 short lines, one realistic micro-scene, only this-lesson vocabulary. It **models the exact capability** (greet → name → ask name → warm response → goodbye) so learners hear the target *in use* before studying it (comprehensible input). "Und dir?" and "auch" are *supporting* words met in context and explained lightly in Vocabulary — not memorization targets, which keeps the load at the core 10.

## STAGE 3 — Vocabulary (~2 min)  *Flip cards, in 2 sets of 5*

Format per card — **Front:** German (+ 🔊). **Back:** English + example + a "watch out" note. Collapsed translation; tap to flip.

### Set 1 — Saying hello & your name
| German | Article/Plural | 🔊 | Meaning | Example | Common beginner confusion |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hallo** | — | HAH-loh | Hello | *Hallo!* | Works any time of day, formal or informal. |
| **Guten Tag** | — | GOO-ten tahk | Hello / Good day (a bit formal) | *Guten Tag!* | Use with strangers/older people; *Hallo* with friends. The *g* in *Tag* sounds like *k*. |
| **Ich heiße …** | *(verb: heißen)* | ish HY-suh | My name is … / I'm called … | *Ich heiße Anna.* | *ß* = "ss". It's **not** "Ich bin heiße". Just *Ich heiße + name*. |
| **Wie heißt du?** | — | vee HYST doo | What's your name? | *Wie heißt du?* | *Wie* = "how" (literally "how are you called"). *du* is informal "you". |
| **Freut mich** | — | froyt mish | Nice to meet you | *Freut mich!* | A fixed phrase — don't translate word-for-word. |

### Set 2 — Being friendly & saying bye
| German | Article/Plural | 🔊 | Meaning | Example | Common beginner confusion |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Wie geht's?** | — | vee gayts | How are you? | *Wie geht's?* | Short for *Wie geht es dir?* Reply with *Gut, danke.* |
| **gut** | — | goot | good / well | *Gut, danke.* | Answers *Wie geht's?* — not "yes". |
| **danke** | — | DAHN-kuh | thank you | *Danke!* | Add it to sound polite: *Gut, danke.* |
| **und** | — | oont | and | *Und dir?* | *Und dir?* = "And you?" — bounce the question back. |
| **Tschüss** | — | CHÜSS | bye (informal) | *Tschüss!* | Casual goodbye. The *ü* is like "ee" with rounded lips. |

**Vocabulary microcopy:** *"Set 1 of 2 — just meet these. You'll practice and review them next."*

**Rationale:** exactly the words the capability needs, **chunked into 5+5** (working memory). Phrases (*Ich heiße…, Freut mich, Wie geht's*) are taught as **whole units** — usable immediately, decomposed later (VOCABULARY_GUIDELINES). Every card names the **specific beginner trap** (ß, formal/informal, "don't translate word-for-word").

## STAGE 4 — Pattern Discovery (~1.5 min)  *Notice → name*

**On-screen — Notice (points back to the dialogue):**
> **Did you notice?** You already saw these two lines:
> • *Ich **heiße** Ben.*
> • *Wie **heißt** du?*
> The same word — *heißen* ("to be called") — changed a little. **Can you spot the change?** *(reveal)*

**On-screen — Name it:**
> **Saying and asking names**
> | Who | Form | Example |
> |---|---|---|
> | **ich** (I) | **heiße** | Ich heiße Anna. |
> | **du** (you) | **heißt** | Wie heißt du? |
>
> **Why:** the ending changes with *who* is speaking — **-e** for *I*, **-t** for *you*. That's all you need today.
> **[ Got it → ]**

**Emma (optional "Explain more"):**
> "Notice it's like a tiny switch: *ich* → *heiß**e***, *du* → *heiß**t***. You'll see this same switch on lots of German verbs later — but today, just those two are enough. 👍"

**Rationale:** **inductive** — the learner *finds* the ich/du switch they already heard, then it's named with a 2-row table and **one** why-line (Noticing Hypothesis + minimal load). No conjugation drill, no jargon ("no V2", "no personal endings paradigm"). Emma offers depth *only on request*.

## STAGE 5 — Guided Practice (~2 min)

The shipped lesson uses **Exercises 1–4** (recognition → matching → ordering → fill-in), then Mini Review. Ex. 5–6 (listening, micro-production) are part of the reference palette; include the micro-production if the learner is engaged.

> **Practice intro (microcopy):** *"Let's try it — no pressure. Wrong answers just help us know what to review."*

**Exercise 1 — Recognition (multiple-choice)**
- **Purpose:** bind meaning to form for the core greeting.
- **Prompt:** *Which one means "Hello"?* → ( **Hallo** ) ( Tschüss ) ( Danke )
- **Expected mistake:** confusing *Tschüss* (bye) with *Hallo*.
- **Feedback:** ✓ "Genau! *Hallo* opens the conversation." · ✗ "Not quite — *Tschüss* is 'bye'. *Hallo* means 'hello'."
- **Emma hint:** *"Think about which word you'd say when you arrive, not when you leave."*

**Exercise 2 — Matching (pairs)**
- **Purpose:** consolidate 4 items via recognition.
- **Prompt:** match German ↔ English: *Hallo · Danke · Tschüss · Freut mich* ↔ *Bye · Hello · Nice to meet you · Thank you*.
- **Expected mistake:** *Freut mich* mismatched (fixed phrase).
- **Feedback:** ✓ inline per pair. ✗ "Almost — *Freut mich* is a set phrase, 'nice to meet you'."
- **Emma hint:** *"Two are easy cognates by sound. Start with the ones you're sure of."*

**Exercise 3 — Ordering (build the line)**
- **Purpose:** first taste of word order in production, fully scaffolded.
- **Prompt:** put in order → [ Ben ] [ heiße ] [ Ich ] → **Ich heiße Ben.**
- **Expected mistake:** *Heiße ich Ben* / *Ich Ben heiße*.
- **Feedback:** ✓ "Perfect — *Ich · heiße · [name]*." · ✗ "Close — start with *Ich*, then *heiße*, then the name."
- **Emma hint:** *"Who first? Start with the person — *Ich*."*

**Exercise 4 — Fill-in (cued production of the pattern)**
- **Purpose:** apply the discovered ich/du switch.
- **Prompt:** *"Wie ___ du?"* → ( heiße ) ( **heißt** ) ( heißen )
- **Expected mistake:** choosing *heiße* (the *ich* form) for *du*.
- **Feedback:** ✓ "Yes — with *du* it's *heißt*." · ✗ "Remember the switch: *ich heiße*, but *du heißt*."
- **Emma hint:** *"You just discovered this — *du* takes the **-t** ending."*

**Exercise 5 — Listening (script only; audio in the live lesson)**
- **Purpose:** bind sound → meaning.
- **Audio script (spoken):** *"Freut mich!"*
- **Prompt:** *What did you hear?* → ( Nice to meet you ) ( Thank you ) ( Goodbye )  · [🔊 replay] [0.5×]
- **Expected mistake:** guessing *Danke* by rhythm.
- **Feedback:** ✓ "That's it — *Freut mich* = 'nice to meet you'." · ✗ "Listen again at 0.5× — it's *Freut mich*."
- **Emma hint:** *"It has two words and a soft 'sh' at the end."*

**Exercise 6 — Micro-production (type it)** *(optional, engagement-dependent)*
- **Purpose:** first free-ish recall of the core sentence.
- **Prompt:** *"Introduce yourself. Type: I'm called [your name]."* → expected **Ich heiße …**
- **Tolerant matching:** accept any name; case/spacing-insensitive; accept *Ich bin …* as a valid alternative (praise it).
- **Expected mistake:** English word order, missing *heiße*.
- **Feedback:** ✓ "*Ich heiße [name]* — you just introduced yourself in German. 🎉" · ✗ "So close — the shape is *Ich heiße [name]*."
- **Emma coaching:** *"Whatever your name is, drop it after *heiße*. There's no wrong name!"*

**Rationale:** the four core exercises walk **recognition → matching → ordering → cued fill-in** (errorless-leaning, high success), with one listening and one gentle production. Every item targets a real element of the capability — **no busywork**. Feedback always names the *why* and never penalizes (FEEDBACK_GUIDELINES).

## STAGE 6 — Speaking (~1.5 min, optional)  *with Emma*

> **On-screen:** *"Try it with Emma — mistakes are how you learn. No mic? Just type. [ Skip for now ]"*

**Emma (opens):** "Hallo! 😊 **Wie heißt du?**" *(suggestion chip: "Ich heiße …")*

- **If learner replies "Ich heiße [name]":** "Freut mich, **[name]**! You just introduced yourself in German. Now ask me — **Wie heißt du?**"
- **If learner replies in English ("I'm Sam"):** "Nice! In German that's *Ich heiße Sam.* Give it a try — I'll wait. 🙂" *(does not reveal more; nudges retry)*
- **If learner is stuck / blank:** *(chip appears)* "No rush — tap *Ich heiße …* and add your name."
- **If learner asks me my name:** "Ich heiße Emma. **Freut mich!** And that's a full first conversation — well done."
- **If learner skips:** "No problem — you can practice anytime. Your words are saved. 👋"

**Coaching philosophy:** **no numeric score, no red 'wrong'.** Emma praises the attempt, models the ideal once, and invites a retry (hint ladder). Pronunciation, if commented on, is encouragement only: *"Nice — that ß came out clean."*

**Rationale:** production **last**, once the learner has the words + pattern; **optional and scaffolded** so no one is blocked or embarrassed (SPEAKING_GUIDELINES, Guided-not-Gated).

## STAGE 7 — Mini Review (~1 min)  *retrieve today's items*

3–4 rapid items over the whole lesson, mixing **recognition + recall**, always ending on a win:
1. **Recognition:** *"How do you say goodbye?"* → ( Hallo ) ( **Tschüss** ) ( Danke )
2. **Recall (cued):** *"Ask someone's name: Wie ___ du?"* → **heißt**
3. **Listening:** *(🔊 "Wie geht's?")* → *What does it mean?* → **How are you?**
4. **Production (light):** *"Reply politely: you're good and you thank them."* → **Gut, danke.**

**Microcopy:** *"Quick check — you've got this."* On completion → **all 10 items seed to SRS** (SM-2, status *New*); Exercise 4/Mini-review misses enter at lower ease.

**Rationale:** the **testing effect** consolidates today's items and ensures tomorrow's recall; it's framed as a friendly check, never a graded quiz.

## Common beginner mistakes (consolidated, for authors/Emma)
| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix in-lesson |
|---|---|---|
| "Ich bin heiße" | Mixing *Ich bin* + *Ich heiße* | Card note + Ordering exercise |
| *Wie heiße du?* | Using the *ich* form with *du* | Pattern Discovery + Fill-in Ex. 4 |
| Translating *Freut mich* literally | It's a fixed phrase | Card "watch out" + Matching Ex. 2 |
| Saying *Tschüss* to a stranger formally | Register unawareness | Card note (Tschüss = casual); *Guten Tag* taught as formal |
| Pronouncing *ß* as "b/beta" | Unfamiliar letter | Respelling "ss" + audio |
| *ch/ü* sounds | New phonemes | 0.5× audio + beginner respelling |

## Assumptions & trade-offs
- **Assumption:** audio (native or high-quality TTS) exists for every German line/word.
- **Trade-off:** teaching *Ich heiße* as a chunk *before* explaining verbs means the learner produces correct German without full understanding — intentional (confidence-first); the ich/du switch is surfaced just enough to be safe.
- **Edge case:** learners who already know "Hallo/Tschüss" (very common) still gain the **pattern** and the **ask-a-name** ability — the lesson isn't wasted on them.
